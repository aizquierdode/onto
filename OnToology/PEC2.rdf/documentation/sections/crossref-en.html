It is one of the best known red grapes in the world. It grows in almost all the major wine-growing areas, in a diverse spectrum of climates, from the Okanagan Valley (Canada) to the Bekaa Valley (Lebanon). Cabernet Sauvignon became famous for its presence in Bordeaux wine, where it is often blended with Merlot and Cabernet Franc. From France, the grape has spread across Europe and the New World, settling in the Santa Cruz Mountains of California, the Napa Valley, Hawkes Bay in New Zealand, the Margaret River and Coonawarra region of Australia, and the valleys Chileans from Maipo and Colchagua. For much of the 20th century, it was the most widely planted premium red wine grape in the world, until it was overtaken by Merlot in the 1990s.

It is a greenish-skinned grape native to the Bordeaux region of France. The grape probably took its name from the French words sauvage ("wild") and blanc ("white"), due to its primitive origins in southwestern France.1 It is possibly a descendant of savagnin. Sauvignon blanc is planted in many countries of the world. It produces a refreshing and dry monovarietal white wine. The grape is also a component of the famous dessert wines of the Sauternes and Barsac AOCs. Sauvignon blanc is widely cultivated in France, Italy, Spain, Argentina, Chile, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, South Africa, and the US states of Washington and California. Some New World Sauvignon Blanc wines, especially Californian ones, may also be called fumé blanc.

It is a red grape variety cultivated extensively to produce full-bodied red wines1 in Spain, where it originates.
